Introduction

Good data-driven questions are at the heart of all data exercises. Questions drive our data collection, focus our analysis and shape our communication. In this session, we get back to basics to learn the art of good questions.

This session includes a series of slides to build your knowledge, and a short quiz to test your knowledge. The expected length of time to complete all segments is 10 minutes.

In this introductory session to formulating questions using examples from the COVID-19 pandemic, you will learn:

  • Why we ask framing questions and why they matter;
  • The elements of good framing questions; and
  • How framing questions shape analysis.

The Role of Questions

01

We Ask Questions to Frame & Collect

As Einstein noted, spending time on the questions makes the work easier of getting the data we need.

We formulate questions to frame our data exercises. These framing questions can be thought of as our research question. They help define the data we need and guide our entire process. Data-driven insight starts with good framing questions.

We also ask questions to collect data. These types of questions help us acquire the data we need to answer our framing question. How we ask these questions shape the answers we get and the resulting data. High quality data starts with good data collection questions.

01

Ask, Answer, Act

Clear framing questions start and bind our data exercises. They are key to ensuring you acquire the data you need, unlock value and insight in that data, and use that insight to drive action. In short, ask, answer, act.

Every data exercise, whether a multi-sector needs assessment, assessing the effectiveness of community engagement activities, or creation of a COVID-19 dashboard is driven by a well-defined question, you are trying to answer.

01

Questions That Frame Data Exercises

A framing question is a fundamental element in any data exercise and also frequently an under-appreciated and overlooked part of the process. Why do framing questions matter?

  • They give our data exercises direction and focus.
  • They inform our actions and outputs at every step of the exercise.
  • They keep data processes aligned to a common reference, making sure we collect only the data we need and produce the type of analysis expected to help us make decisions.

01

Good Questions Anchor & Clarify

Questions anchor your data exercise and help you find the answers you need to make data informed decisions. To do this well, questions should be:

  • Specific enough to tell us what needs to be analysed, where, when and on what scale;
  • Relevant to an objective and focused on what needs to be understood in order to make a decision;
  • Comprehensive of everything that needs to be understood in order to make that decision. We do not want to be left still seeking answers; and
  • Realistic, that, given data availability and resource constraints, what we want to know is possible to know?

Getting to the right question can take time, but is time well spent.

01

From Broad Concepts to Specific Measures

We often start a data exercise with a broad concepts we want to explore but it can be hard to know what data we need to better understand the concept. First, we may want to define the different dimensions of the concept that we want to explore. We can then determine indicators that can shine light on and help us understand those dimensions. Once we have gone through this process, it is easier to hone in on the specific data needed to measure those indicators indicators.

The Role of Questions in Data Analysis

We use data to help humanitarians make better decisions with relevant information. There are different categories of analysis, each of which is used to answer different types of questions.

  • Descriptive analysis answers the question of what happened in the past.
  • Explanatory analysis answers the question of why something happened in the past.
  • Interpretive analysis answers the question of what is salient.
  • Predictive analysis answers the question of what is most likely to happen in the future.
  • Prescriptive analysis answers the question of what we should do about it.

The question you ask depends on many factors including the audience of the final product, how the results will be used, and the data, time and resources available.

02

What Happened?

At times, when we look to data for an answer, we simply want to know what happened. These are the types of descriptive questions that many COVID-19 dashboards and visualisations have been designed to help answer.

  1. How many cases of COVID-19 have been reported?
  2. Are the numbers of cases increasing or decreasing?

Descriptive questions ask to compare and contrast data to identify patterns, trends, and anomalies or outliers. They help us explore and understand what has happened in the past.

02

The Importance of Descriptive Analysis

Descriptive analysis help users understand a topic and its various facets but do not attempt to provide an explanation or interpretation. The interactive dashboards and visualisations that we see in the humanitarian sector are often examples of descriptive analysis. These types of products help to summerise, compare and contrast data but leave the user to draw their own conclusions.

02

Why Did it Happen?

We may need more from our data than a summary. Often knowing what happened is important but insufficient to support decision making. Explanatory questions can guide us in digging deeper by asking the analyst to investigate causal relationships, identify underlying factors, and seek a deeper understanding of why something is happening.

We ask explanatory questions when we want to uncover associations, correlations, connections and relationships between data points and may ask for an interpretation of the data to draw conclusions.

02

Explanatory Analysis

Explanatory data anlysis builds on descriptive anlysis. For example, an explanatory data question could ask about how a given non-pharmaceutical intervention has impacted the spread of the virus.

02

What is the Most Important?

Knowing how much, how many or what happened can serve as a foundation for when we need to know what is most important. Interpretive questions ask us to prioritise, rate or rank. We use these type of questions when we want to know what is essential and why. In short, they ask to interpret from an array of data what, where or who, we need to focus on.

A typical example of this type of question would be:

  1. In which countries is the situation the most severe?
  2. Which countries are deteriorating the fastest/deepest?

This example question is asking to consider a number of factors that have been described, to judge or determine what countries are a priority or the most important.

02

What is Most Likely to Happen Next?

Predictive questions ask to extend current conditions to forecast future outcomes, to develop alternative futures or to identify triggers. These questions seek to anticipate what could happen next, to take preemptive or preparedness action.

Within the COVID-19 context, this could mean developing predictive models to help better understand the expected scale or duration of the crisis (i.e. the total number of cases, severe cases and deaths) in specific locations. It could also mean developing models to assess how different response interventions can be expected to impact the epidemic curve.

02

What Should We Do?

Prescriptive questions ask for advice and suggestion on policy, courses of action, etc. They may ask for a close examination of the potential effect of future decisions and consideration of alternate actions and their outcomes. Prescriptive questions can be underpinned by the answers to previous questions asking to describe, explain and interpret a situation.

Example: Where are humanitarian operations stopped/interrupted due to COVID-19? What is the result? What can we do to mitigate the interruptions?
